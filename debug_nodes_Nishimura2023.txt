## Introduction
Proximal hamstring injuries are common in athletes and are often sustained during rapid hip flexion and knee extension movements during sports activities.[^1] Anatomically, the proximal hamstring originates from the lateral aspect of the ischial tuberosity and is composed of the semimembranosus, semitendinosus, and long head of the biceps femoris.[^18] The treatment for proximal hamstring injury can be broadly divided into nonoperative therapy and surgical treatment. Surgical repair is required for patients with 2 or 3 tendon avulsions with retraction of ≥2 cm, or when symptoms do not improve after 6 months of nonoperative therapy.[^2] Although treatment of properly diagnosed cases in the acute phase is relatively easy, some patients develop a chronic course due to primary misdiagnosis or failure of initial treatment with nonoperative therapy.

Hamstring injuries in the chronic phase are more challenging to treat surgically because the hamstring tendon is often greatly retracted. A systematic review and meta-analysis performed by Bodendorfer et al [^2] reported that the acute repair group had higher patient satisfaction, less pain, restored muscle strength, and higher functional scores, whereas the chronic repair group had a higher incidence of sciatica and neurological complications. It has also been reported that surgical reconstruction or augmentation with grafts is often required in cases of chronic complete avulsion with retraction >5 cm.[^22] The surgical reconstructions that use the 3 most common graft types, Achilles tendon,[^6] [^9] [^17] [^20] [^22] distal hamstring,[^4] [^19] and fascia lata,[^14] [^24] have been reported to achieve satisfactory postoperative clinical outcomes. However, no previous studies have evaluated these reconstructive procedures from a biomechanical perspective, and in the absence of biomechanical literature supporting a stronger time-zero reconstruction strategy, treatment planning and postoperative rehabilitation have not been optimized to allow for a faster return to sports activity.

By testing the time-zero characteristics of these reconstructive procedures and comparing the results to the native condition, clinicians may gain better insight into graft selection and postrehabilitation management strategies for the surgical treatment of chronic proximal hamstring injuries to accelerate return to sports activity and improve the return-to-sports rate.

Reconstruction with Achilles tendon allografts is the most widely used of these reconstructive techniques, and its postoperative clinical outcomes have been reported frequently [^6] [^17] [^20] [^22]; however, this technique can only be performed in regions where allografts are available. On the other hand, distal hamstring grafts and fascia lata grafts are available as autografts and can be used in all regions. Yet, reports of clinical outcomes after reconstructive surgery using these grafts are lacking.[^4] [^14] [^24]

The purpose of this study was to (1) compare the failure load and elongation to failure between the proximal hamstring tendon reconstruction with distal hamstring grafts and the reconstruction with fascia lata grafts and (2) compare the stiffness between these 2 reconstructions and the native state. We hypothesized that reconstruction with a distal hamstring graft would be stronger than reconstruction with a fascia lata graft and biomechanically equivalent to a native tendon in terms of stiffness.

### Specimen Preparation

Seven pairs of fresh-frozen human cadaveric hemipelvises for a total of 14 specimens (mean age, 60.4 ± 5.0 years; range, 51-65 years; 6 male, 1 female) with no evidence of previous injury or abnormality were used in this study. Funding for this study was internally provided by our institution. The specimens used were donated to a tissue bank for medical research and then purchased by our institution. The use of cadaveric specimens does not require institutional review board approval at our institution.

Specimens were placed in a prone position, and exposure of the proximal hamstring insertions was achieved using an open approach. After reflection of the gluteus maximus medially and superiorly from its femoral insertion, the semimembranosus, semitendinosus, and long head of the biceps femoris were followed proximally to their respective insertions on the ischial tuberosity. Upon visualization and isolation of the proximal attachments, all other soft tissue was removed from the ischial tuberosity. A bone saw was then used to make cuts at the inferior pubic ramus and ischial body, slightly inferior to the ischial spine at the level of the lesser sciatic notch, to allow for safe removal and full preservation of the hamstring and bony attachment site. Dissected specimens were stored at −20°C until the day before testing. Specimens were thawed 24 hours before biomechanical testing, and the ischial tuberosity was potted in a cylindrical mold using polymethyl methacrylate (Fricke Dental).

Each pair of specimens was randomly assigned to undergo 2 repairs. One specimen of the pair was repaired using a distal hamstring allograft (distal hamstring graft group), and the other specimen of the pair was repaired using a fascia lata allograft (fascia lata graft group).

### Surgical Technique

The surgery of the reconstruction with distal hamstring graft and the reconstruction with fascia lata graft in the present study was performed following the technique of previous studies.[^4] [^14] As the cadavers did not include distal hamstring, distal hamstring grafts were collected from different cadavers, and fascia lata grafts were collected from the same cadavers. All surgeries were performed by 2 fellowship-trained orthopaedic surgeons (H.N. and K.Y.). For the reconstruction with distal hamstring graft, the 2 distal hamstring tendon grafts (10-cm length each) were passed through the proximal tendon mass at 1 and 2 cm distal to its proximal end, with the more proximal one running in a medial-lateral direction and the more distal one running in a ventral-dorsal direction, creating a 90-90 configuration. The proximal ends of grafts were then whipstitched to themselves. Krakow sutures [^12] with No. 2 high-strength suture (FiberWire; Arthrex) were then used to oppose the individual graft bundles, creating a quadruple hamstring neo tendon. Two 5.5-mm fully threaded suture anchors (Bio-Corkscrew; Arthrex) were placed 1 cm apart on the lateral ischial footprint. Four strands of sutures from the anchors were passed through the proximal end of the neotendon. Then, 2 of those strands were passed through each of the two 5.5-mm fully threaded, knotless suture anchors (SwiveLock; Arthrex). The knotless suture anchors were introduced to the posterior ischium just medial to the repaired tendon mass, and then a suture bridge repair was performed.

For the reconstruction with fascia lata graft, a 5 × 15–cm graft was used. Three 2.8-mm double-loaded all-suture anchors (Q-fix; Smith & Nephew) were placed 1 cm apart on the lateral ischial footprint, and 1 thread for each anchor was removed. All 3 strands of suture from the anchor were passed through the fascia lata graft at half the length of the graft and tied to fix the graft.

### Biomechanical Testing

The potted ischial tuberosity was secured within a custom fixture that was clamped to the base platform of a dynamic tensile testing system (Instron ElectroPuls E10000; Instron Systems) with the musculotendinous portion of the hamstring fixed within a custom clamp fixed to the end effector ([Figure 2](#fig2-03635465231206464)). The dynamic tensile testing machine can reach a maximum load of 10,000 N with a resolution of 0.1 N, and the displacement gauge can move up to 60 mm with a resolution of 0.0001 mm. Pilot testing confirmed that there was no noticeable slipping at the clamps when the applied load exceeded 300 N. Specimens were consistently clamped between 5 and 10 cm distal to the ischial enthesis. Applied loads were representative of in vivo loads, with the applied loading direction resulting in a shear force between the hamstring entheses and the ischial tuberosity.

![](https://journals.sagepub.com/cms/10.1177/03635465231206464/asset/aa4739c2-925d-4676-b37f-4ad6f6e67d21/assets/images/large/10.1177_03635465231206464-fig2.jpg)

Figure 2. (Left) Side view and (right) front view of the biomechanical testing setup. Potted ischial tuberosity secured to the base of the dynamic tensile testing system, and hamstring fixed within a clamp that is secured to the end effector of the dynamic tensile testing system.

All specimens were initially tested in the native state. Specimens were preconditioned using a series of cyclic loads representative of a progressive postoperative rehabilitation protocol. Schache et al [^25] reported that the maximum normalized net force on the hamstring during walking reaches 1600 N for an 80-kg individual. Recognizing the time-zero nature of the study, we applied a 1-Hz cyclic loading protocol to the specimen with loads cycling from 25 N to a maximum load in 75-N increments every 50 cycles.

Recognizing the time-zero nature of the study, we applied a 1-Hz cyclic loading protocol to the specimen with loads cycling from 25 N to a maximum load in 75-N increments every 50 cycles. The specimen was then pulled at 1 mm/s for 20 mm, and then the applied load was returned to 25 N. In this way, native stiffness could be calculated, and specimen damage could be avoided. Stiffness was calculated as the slope of the linear region of the load-elongation curve. After performing the repairs, we applied the same cyclic loading protocol. If the specimen did not fail after reaching 375 N when testing the repair state, then the specimen was pulled at 1 mm/s to failure. To account for reduced time-zero loading, 325 N, 65% of the 500-N in vivo rectus femoris load [^25] was selected. The failure load, elongation at failure, stiffness, and mode of failure were recorded for each specimen. Stiffness as a percentage of the stiffness of the native state was calculated for both repair groups.

### Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using commercial software (MATLAB; MathWorks), and data were expressed as mean ± SD. First, Shapiro-Wilk tests were used to verify normal distribution of ultimate failure load, elongation at failure, and stiffness data. Failure load and elongation at failure were compared between repair groups using Wilcoxon signed-rank test, as these data were not normally distributed and distributed symmetrically about the median value. Paired *t* test was used to compare stiffness data between repair groups and between the repair state and the native state within each repair group as these data were normally distributed. Statistical significance was defined as *P* ≤.05.
